Abstract

Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities not only in language generation but also in advancing scientific discovery. A growing body of work has explored ways to improve their reasoning, from self-consistency and chain-of-thought to multi-agent debate. Inspired by the dynamics of scientific committees and The Society of Mind, we introduce Roundtable Policy, a complementary inference-time reasoning framework that performs inference through the weighted consensus of multiple LLMs. Our findings indicate that this approach significantly enhances reasoning in complex heterogeneous scientific tasks and improves scientific narratives in terms of creativity, rigor, and logical coherence, while reducing hallucinations that single models are prone to. Our approach emphasizes structured and interpretable consensus rather than opaque convergence, while requires only black-box access and uniform procedures, making it broadly applicable to multi-LLM reasoning.

Benchmark Results

We compare Roundtable Policy (RT) with individual LLM baselines in both MultiTask and SingleTask settings. Overall results are summarized in the benchmark table, with relative gains shown in the improvement figures.

In MultiTask, RT achieves the highest average score on 8 out of 9 tasks and an average lift of +13.01%. The only exception is Task 009: Biology, where Qwen-plus (Player 008, 51.64 ± 6.57) and GPT-4o (Player 001, 51.53 ± 6.64) slightly outperform RT (47.55 ± 7.02). On reasoning-intensive tasks, RT delivers large improvements, such as +90.48% over Gemini-2.0 (Player 009, Task 007: Math) and +52.07% over Claude-3.7 Sonnet (Player 006, Task 006: Semiconductor).

In SingleTask (scientific proposal writing), RT achieves the best scores in all nine rubric dimensions—Creativity, Scientific Rigor, and Logical Coherence across Background, Methodology, and Impact. Average performance reaches 90–91% with narrow confidence intervals. The largest gains appear in Impact–Scientific Rigor, confirming that RT consistently elevates weaker models (e.g., GPT-4o-mini, Player 006) while maintaining or improving strong ones (e.g., Claude-3.7 Sonnet, Player 008). Overall, RT delivers a +11.04% average lift.

Ablation Results

To better understand the contributions of our design choices, we conduct two ablation studies. Both highlight how Roundtable Policy (RT) advances scientific reasoning in structured benchmarks and improves narrative quality in open-ended writing.

Policy Ablation. We compare RT with a Best-Single-Model Policy (BSMP), which always selects the response from the single model with the highest confidence weight instead of fusing multiple players. Results show that RT consistently outperforms BSMP: in MultiTask, RT achieves higher accuracy on 8 of 9 scientific tasks (especially in reasoning-heavy domains such as mathematics and mechanics), while Task 009: Biology remains challenging for both. In SingleTask (proposal writing), RT dominates all nine rubric dimensions, yielding both higher average scores and lower variance. These results confirm that consensus fusion—integrating diverse perspectives—is essential, whereas relying on a single “winner” is insufficient.

Rounds Ablation. We also study the effect of training rounds used to construct the confidence-weight table. In MultiTask, performance improves steadily with more rounds, stabilizing near 1000 rounds. In contrast, SingleTask converges quickly: fewer than 100 rounds already surpass baselines, and additional rounds bring diminishing returns. This shows that heterogeneous benchmarks require long-run calibration of model reliability, while rubric-based proposal writing stabilizes much faster.

Key Insight: Weighted consensus is fundamentally more effective than selecting the best single model, and the stability of the confidence-weight table depends on task structure. Together, these findings underscore the importance of consensus-driven fusion for advancing both scientific reasoning and scientific narrative.
